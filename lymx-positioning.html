About · Plain-English positioning

LYMX is rewards. Not money, not currency, not crypto.

A clean explanation of what LYMX actually is and what it isn't, designed to clear up confusion before it starts. If you've ever wondered "wait, is this like Bitcoin?" — this page answers it.

The 30-second version

LYMX is a shared loyalty rewards program for local Businesses. Like a coffee shop punch card, but the punch card works at every Business in the network instead of just one.

You earn LYMX when you buy from a participating Business. You redeem LYMX for $0.01 in goods or services per LYMX at any other participating Business.

It's not money. It's not a currency. It's not crypto. It's not an investment. It's a promotional credit unit — useful only for redemption at participating Businesses, with a fixed redemption value.

✅ What LYMX is

  • A loyalty rewards programLike Starbucks Rewards, airline miles, or a punch card — but shared across many local Businesses instead of one.
  • A promotional credit unit1 LYMX = $0.01 of redemption value at any participating Business. Fixed, never floats.
  • Earned through purchasesYou earn LYMX when you spend with a Business that participates. Like points on a credit card.
  • Redeemable for goods + servicesYou can get $0.01 of merchandise or service per LYMX. Cannot be converted to cash.
  • Network-wideEarn at one Business, redeem at any other in the network. The shared part is the innovation.
  • Free to useCustomers don't pay anything to join or hold LYMX. Businesses pay a subscription.

❌ What LYMX is NOT

  • Not money or legal tenderYou can't pay rent, taxes, or general bills with LYMX. It's only redeemable at participating Businesses for goods/services.
  • Not a currencyIt doesn't trade against the dollar. It has a fixed redemption value, doesn't float, doesn't get exchanged on markets.
  • Not cryptocurrencyNo blockchain, no mining, no tokens. It's a database entry in a centralized loyalty system, like Starbucks Rewards.
  • Not a security or investmentYou can't buy LYMX as an investment. Doesn't appreciate. Designed specifically NOT to be a store of value.
  • Not a stored monetary balanceYour LYMX wallet doesn't hold money. It tracks a redemption right against participating Businesses.
  • Not convertible to cashYou can't withdraw your LYMX as dollars. Only redeem for goods/services at network Businesses.
  • Not a stablecoin or digital assetDespite having a fixed redemption value, it's structured as a loyalty program, not a financial product.
  • Not transferable between peopleLYMX can't be sent, gifted, traded, or transferred to another wallet or account. It stays with the wallet that earned it and only redeems at participating Businesses. LYMX is issued and redeemed solely by the LYMX platform.

Why this matters — the regulatory positioning

The way a loyalty program is structured determines what regulations apply to it. We chose a structure that places LYMX firmly in "loyalty rewards" territory and explicitly OUT of "money / securities / crypto" territory. This protects everyone.

Three structural choices that keep LYMX as rewards, not money

  • Fixed redemption value, no appreciation. 1 LYMX = $0.01 redemption value, today and forever. If the value floated upward, LYMX would become an investment. By locking it, customers spend rather than hoard.
  • Redeemable only at participating Businesses, not for cash. If LYMX could be cashed out, it would become a stored monetary balance subject to money-transmitter regulation. By limiting redemption to goods/services at participating Businesses, it stays in loyalty territory.
  • No secondary market and no transfers. LYMX can't be transferred, gifted, traded, bought, or sold between people at all — there is no peer-to-peer movement and no exchange. Only the LYMX platform issues and redeems it. With no transferability and no secondary market, no security analysis is triggered.

What it means in practice

  • For customers: Same protections as a punch card or rewards program. Not FDIC-insured (because it's not a deposit). Not subject to securities laws (because it's not an investment).
  • For Businesses: Participating in LYMX is no different than running your own loyalty program from a regulatory standpoint. No money-transmitter license required.
  • For the platform (us): We don't have to register as a money services business, broker-dealer, or crypto exchange. We're a loyalty program platform, like Square Loyalty or Toast Loyalty — just with shared redemption.
If a unit can appreciate, it stops being a reward and becomes an investment. People hoard investments — they don't redeem them. That's why we locked the value.

"But wait — what about [specific feature]?"

"You have a digital wallet. Doesn't that make it crypto?"

The Starbucks app has a digital wallet too. So does Sephora's Beauty Insider. So does United MileagePlus. "Digital wallet" describes a software interface — it doesn't determine the underlying nature of what's stored.

"Can you send LYMX to friends? Isn't that like Venmo?"

No — and that's deliberate. LYMX is not transferable between people: you can't send, gift, trade, or buy it. There is no peer-to-peer movement, which is the opposite of Venmo. LYMX is issued and redeemed only by the LYMX platform. To bring a friend in, you use a referral link — they sign up and earn their own LYMX. Because there's no transfer mechanism at all, LYMX stays firmly in loyalty-rewards territory and well outside money-transmission.

"You have a fixed redemption value of $0.01. Isn't that a stablecoin?"

A stablecoin is structured to maintain peg through reserves, redeemable for the underlying currency. LYMX has no reserve mechanism, can't be redeemed for cash, and isn't a coin in any technical sense — it's a database entry. Same as a Starbucks Reward star (worth $0.16 per star).

"Businesses settle in dollars. Doesn't that make LYMX a payment system?"

When a customer redeems LYMX at Business A that was earned at Business B, our settlement system reconciles between the two Businesses' accounts in dollars. That's accounting infrastructure — not customer-facing payments. The customer doesn't see or interact with the dollar settlement; they just redeem LYMX for goods.

Final note on regulatory positioning: We've structured LYMX in good faith as a loyalty program based on legal review. State + federal financial regulations evolve, and a regulator could in the future re-classify LYMX. If that happened, we'd update these definitions and notify all users. For now: rewards. Not money. Not crypto. Not securities.

FAQ

Could LYMX become a currency or crypto in the future?

No plans to. Our entire structure is designed AROUND not being a currency. Doing so would change the regulatory profile substantially and isn't something we'd undertake lightly.

What if LYMX shuts down — can I get my balance as cash?

No, but yes — let me explain. LYMX balance can't be converted to cash by design. If LYMX shuts down, the wind-down protocol gives customers an extended redemption window (90+ days) to spend remaining LYMX at participating Businesses. After that window, residual balances are donated to a charitable cause. See trust-and-safety.

Why don't you just call it a punch card?

"Punch card" implies single-Business redemption. LYMX is multi-Business. We use "shared loyalty rewards" or "loyalty rewards network" to capture the difference.

Are LYMX rewards taxable?

For customers: generally no. Loyalty rewards earned in the course of personal purchases are typically not taxable income (per IRS rev rul 76-96 etc.). For Businesses: LYMX-related expenses are deductible as marketing & promotions. See biz-tax-guide.

If I'm a Partner, are my commissions taxable?

Yes — Partner commissions are 1099 income. See partner-tax-guide.
