Case study · Restaurant

How a 38-seat restaurant filled Tuesday nights and added $3,800/mo in revenue.

Oakline Kitchen, a farm-to-table restaurant two blocks from Brew & Bean, joined LYMX as a Founding 25 Business in February 2026. Eight weeks later: their slow nights were full, their late-table-turn problem was solved, and they had a steady stream of new customers from coffee shops and bookstores nearby.

Daniel Whitley opened Oakline Kitchen in 2022, betting that Las Vegas was ready for elevated farm-to-table beyond the Strip. Three years later, he had a small loyal following, a glowing review in Las Vegas Magazine, and a chronic problem: Tuesday and Wednesday nights were dead. Friday and Saturday were packed; the rest of the week he was burning rent.

The setup before LYMX

Daniel's marketing stack

  • $420/mo on Yelp Ads for the restaurant listing
  • $200/mo on Klaviyo for monthly newsletter (650 subs)
  • $150/mo on Instagram boosting
  • 2 third-party delivery apps (DoorDash + UberEats) at 28% commission per order
  • Total: $770/mo in marketing + ~$3,200/mo in delivery commissions

The problem

  • Tuesday/Wednesday: 8–14 covers (38-seat restaurant — 24% utilization)
  • Friday/Saturday: 75–95 covers across two seatings (200%+ rotation)
  • Marketing was driving NEW customers but not filling SLOW nights
  • Couldn't justify a third hire because of weekday slowness
  • Yelp Ad attribution was unclear at best
"I was good at filling Saturdays. I had no idea how to fill Tuesdays. Yelp didn't help — the new customers it sent came on weekends, when I didn't need them. The problem wasn't acquisition. It was demand-shaping." Daniel Whitley, Chef-owner

Why he tried LYMX

Sarah at Brew & Bean (two blocks down) joined as Founding 25 Business #1 and mentioned the network economics over coffee. Daniel was skeptical of loyalty programs in general — restaurants and points have a fraught history. The thing that flipped him was the multiplier mechanic.

"Sarah explained that she could run a 3× LYMX multiplier on Wednesdays only. That's a tool I didn't have. Cancel the Yelp Ads, replace them with this — even if all I did was pull Tuesday/Wednesday from the dead, that's a win." Daniel Whitley, Chef-owner

The 8-week rollout

Week 1 · Feb 14, 2026

Sign-up + setup

$850 sign-up + $200/mo. Cancelled Yelp Ads ($420/mo savings) and IG boosting ($150/mo savings) immediately. Kept Klaviyo & delivery apps in place to isolate the variable.

Week 2

First multiplier promo: 3× LYMX, Tuesday + Wednesday only

Set the multiplier in promotions. Posted on Instagram once: "Tuesday + Wednesday now 3× LYMX. Yes, ALL menu items." Cost: ~$0 in marketing.

Week 3

First Tuesday lift

Tuesday Feb 24: 22 covers, up from 11 the previous Tuesday. 8 of them were LYMX customers; 4 of those were redemptions from Brew & Bean. Daniel called Sarah that night to confirm.

Week 4–5

Pattern stabilizes

Tuesday/Wednesday counts settle at 18–24 covers (vs 8–14 before). Friday/Saturday traffic slightly UP, not down — meaning LYMX wasn't cannibalizing weekend demand, it was creating new midweek demand.

Week 6

Hit break-even on monthly cost

Incremental Tuesday/Wednesday revenue from new + repeat customers: $3,200/mo. Subtract LYMX wholesale + subscription ($240) — net +$2,960. Comfortably positive.

Week 8 · Apr 11, 2026

Cancelled Klaviyo + cut UberEats

Klaviyo replaced by the LYMX in-wallet engagement system. UberEats cut because the delivery commissions were higher than the LYMX wholesale rate AND the LYMX customers were dine-in. Net savings: $200/mo Klaviyo + ~$1,400/mo UberEats commissions.

What surprised him

Three things, in his words.

1. The customers from other Businesses had a different conversion profile. When someone redeemed LYMX they earned at Brew & Bean, they were 3× more likely to be a first-time visitor than someone who walked in cold. They'd already heard about Oakline through Sarah's customer base — they were warm, not cold, leads. The conversion rate to a second visit was 64%, vs. 28% for walk-ins.

2. The LYMX wholesale rate is way cheaper than delivery commissions. The math: 5 LYMX per dollar of customer spend × $0.008 wholesale = 4¢ per dollar. UberEats charges 28% per order. Even with multipliers running at 2× or 3× on certain nights, LYMX was 8× cheaper than delivery. After realizing this, cutting UberEats was easy.

3. The dashboard surfaced patterns he'd missed. Tuesday/Wednesday weren't just "low traffic" — they were "low traffic at specific 7–9 PM hours." The 5–7 PM was actually fine. Once he saw the hourly heatmap, he changed the multiplier to apply only to 7 PM+ tickets, which doubled the impact per LYMX issued.

"I came in to fix Tuesdays. I left understanding my whole demand pattern. The data was the unexpected gift." Daniel Whitley, Chef-owner
