The boutique fitness death-loop

Andre Lucia opened CycleHaus Studio in Summerlin — 28 bikes, 4 instructors, classes from 5:30am Bootcamp to 7pm Yoga Flow. By 2025, the studio had a death-loop familiar to every boutique fitness owner: they got people in for one class, then 58% never came back.

The reason wasn't the workout (NPS was 9.1). It wasn't pricing ($28/class or $200/10-pack). It was the lack of a habit-formation mechanism. New riders meant well, but life got in the way after the first class, and they drifted.

"I'd see someone walk out after their first class glowing — endorphins, smile, said 'I'll be back!' — and 60% of them, I'd never see again. It wasn't the workout. It was that nothing pulled them back."— Andre Lucia, owner

What changed with LYMX

Andre joined LYMX in February 2026. He'd heard about the network from another studio owner. The pivotal insight came in his second meeting with his Partner Sienna: boutique fitness needs habit-formation, not just loyalty rewards.

Together they designed a 3-stage retention system using LYMX as the lever:

The CycleHaus retention sequence

Class 1
First-class welcome: Customer earns 350 LYMX (the standard 5/$1 on a $28 drop-in + 200 first-visit bonus). Andre's email auto-sends 4 hours later: "Welcome — you've got 350 LYMX. Book class 2 in the next 7 days, earn another 250 bonus."
Class 2
The bridge: Customer takes class 2 within 7 days → auto-rewarded 250 LYMX. Now they have 750+ LYMX = $7.50 toward class-pack discount. This 250 bonus is what saved 58% drop-off → 22%.
Class 3
The lock-in: By class 3, customer's LYMX balance is ~1,100 LYMX. Andre sends "Use 1,000 LYMX off your first 10-pack." Class-pack purchases jumped 35%.
Month 2
Habit lock: Customer has paid for a 10-pack. Each class burns 1 pack-credit + earns 200 LYMX. Customer is now in flywheel: classes → LYMX → class-pack discount → more classes.

The cross-redemption pull

Andre also benefited from LYMX network inflows. About 8% of new CycleHaus members in Q2 2026 were LYMX customers who'd earned LYMX at OTHER Businesses (cafés, salons, restaurants), then redeemed at CycleHaus to try a class.

"I had a customer come in with $40 of LYMX from places she'd been all year. Used it for her first class. Loved it. Bought a 10-pack. She's now a 4x/week regular. That customer cost me $2 in promo bonus to convert. CAC at LYMX is unbeatable."— Andre Lucia

The class-pack math

LYMX changed how CycleHaus customers think about pricing. Before, a $200 10-pack felt like a commitment. With ~1,000 LYMX from their first 2-3 classes acting as a $10 discount, the pack became "$190 — basically a freebie class on me." Mental accounting matters.

Class-pack repurchase rate (purchasing a second 10-pack after the first) jumped from 47% to 64%. Customers who buy a second 10-pack are 8x more likely to be year-round regulars.

Andre's 5 fitness-specific LYMX tactics

  1. The class-2 bonus. The single most important retention lever for boutique fitness. Pay anything to get them to class 2.
  2. Class-pack-discount via LYMX. Reframe pack as "use your LYMX as the down payment." Reduces commitment friction.
  3. Class-streak rewards. "Take 3 classes this week → 500 LYMX bonus." Rewards habit-building.
  4. Monday motivation 2x LYMX. Mondays are slow in fitness. 2x LYMX every Monday turns dead time into demand.
  5. Birthday class-pack gift. 1,000 LYMX for the birthday week. Customers who feel celebrated stay forever.

What it cost CycleHaus

Total LYMX cost over 6 months: ~$1,400 ($850 sign-up + $199/mo for months 4-6 + ~$50/mo issuance cost). For incremental revenue of $67,200 (6 × $11,200), that's a 48× ROI.

Andre's most valuable observation: LYMX gets cheaper over time on a per-customer basis as the network grows. New customers from cross-redemption now cost $0 in marketing — they came to him through other Businesses' LYMX.
