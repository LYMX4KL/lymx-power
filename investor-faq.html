Traction 3 questions

What's your current traction?

As of April 2026: 218 active Businesses (Las Vegas), 14,820 active Customers, 38% MoM customer growth, $184k monthly LYMX volume, 100% Launch 25 Business retention, NPS +62 (across all customers). See live stats for current figures. Detailed cohort analyses in the data room.

What's the growth pattern looking like?

Network effects becoming visible. Cohort retention has improved month over month (m1: 84%, m2: 91%, m3: 94%). Cross-Business referrals — customers earning at one Business and redeeming at another — went from 8% of transactions in month 1 to 31% by month 3. This is the curve we needed to see.

What metrics are you most proud of?

(1) The Launch 25 retention rate — 0 churn after 90 days, when the loyalty industry average for new Business sign-ups is ~22% within 90 days. (2) The cross-Business redemption velocity — proof that the network mechanic works in practice. (3) Partner program efficiency — our average Partner CAC for a new Business is ~$140 versus industry-standard CAC for SaaS small-Business loyalty of $400–700.

Market opportunity 3 questions

How big is the addressable market?

~5.4M small Businesses in the US that would benefit from a shared loyalty network (food, retail, services in walkable urban areas). At $200/mo subscription + ~$30/mo wholesale, that's a $14.9B TAM at full penetration. Realistic 5-year SAM: 50,000 Businesses × $200/mo × 12 = $120M ARR. We've modeled this as a city-by-city expansion, with ~150 cities suitable for LYMX-style network density.

Who's the competition?

Three categories: (1) Single-store loyalty programs (Square Loyalty, Toast Loyalty, Belly, Fivestars) — large but solving a different problem. (2) Coalitions (Plenti — failed; PayPal Honey — limited). (3) Discovery platforms (Yelp, OpenTable) — pay-for-traffic, no loyalty mechanic. None of them combine cross-Business loyalty with point-of-sale integration the way LYMX does. Detailed competitive matrix in the data room.

Why hasn't this been built before?

It has — Plenti tried in 2015 and failed. We've studied that failure carefully. Their failure mode was (1) too many massive partners (American Express, ExxonMobil) without local density, (2) value proposition wasn't clear to consumers, (3) no point-of-sale integration meant customers had to remember to use it. LYMX flips all three: local density first, $0.01 fixed value, native POS integration. Different game.

Unit economics 3 questions

What's your unit economics on a Business?

Revenue per Business: $200/mo subscription + ~$0.002 margin × LYMX volume (~$30 in network margin per active Business per month). Net revenue: ~$230/mo per active Business. Direct COGS per Business: ~$45/mo (support, settlement, ACH). Net contribution margin per Business: ~$185/mo. Assumed 30% churn over 24 months gives an LTV/CAC ratio of ~9× when CAC is ~$140 (Partner-channel) or ~3× when CAC is direct sales ($400). Detailed in the data room.

How does the wholesale rate margin work?

Businesses pay $0.008 per LYMX issued. Customers redeem at $0.01. The $0.002 spread is what funds the network. At 20M LYMX issued/year network-wide (roughly month 12 projection), that's $40k/year in network margin. Scales linearly with network volume — and is structurally protected by the fact that LYMX redemption is fixed.

When do you expect to be cash-flow positive?

Month 28 in our base case. Month 22 in our optimistic case (faster Phoenix + Reno ramp). The bottleneck is launch cost per city — each city requires ~6 months of pre-launch ops + sales work before reaching break-even. We're prioritizing density-first expansion, which means 4-6 cities at full density rather than 30 cities at low density.

Risks 3 questions

What are the biggest risks to the business?

Top 3: (1) Network density failure — if a city doesn't reach critical mass (we've internally targeted 50+ Businesses in 6 months), the cross-Business referral mechanic breaks, and we look like a single-store loyalty program with extra steps. (2) POS integration challenges — Toast, Square, and Clover hold significant power over our access. We mitigate via direct partnerships and a public API + CSV fallback. (3) Regulatory risk — the LYMX Partner program structure is referral-based but could face Multi-Level-Marketing-style scrutiny. We've structured carefully (no buy-in, FTC-compliant disclosures, no investment language) and have outside legal review of every Partner-facing page.

What if Square or Toast launches a competing product?

Real risk. Both have the existing Business relationship + the technical capability. Two factors mitigate: (1) Cross-platform support — neither Square nor Toast has incentive to support each other's Businesses. We do, by design. (2) Network neutrality — Businesses don't trust their POS provider with cross-merchant data. They might trust an independent. (3) Speed — they're large companies; their decision cycles are 18+ months. We have a 12-month head start before this risk becomes acute.

What's the regulatory risk on the Partner program?

Partner program is structured as a referral commission program — no buy-in, no inventory, no recruiting requirements, no tiers contingent on downline size. FTC-compliant income disclosure on all Partner-facing pages. Outside counsel review every 6 months. Risk is low but not zero — we monitor FTC guidance and adjust.

Exit strategy 2 questions

What does a successful exit look like?

Likely paths: (1) Strategic acquisition by a large POS company (Toast, Square, Clover) — most likely. (2) Acquisition by a payments company (Stripe, Visa, MasterCard) interested in the loyalty mechanic. (3) Acquisition by a major retailer for white-label use. (4) IPO at scale — possible but requires ~$100M ARR. Comparable transactions: Plenti (acquired by AmEx for ~$80M before pivot), Square Loyalty (Square acquired Eastside Loyalty for ~$25M in 2018), Belly (acquired by Tiger Pistol for ~$30M in 2019). Realistic exit range: $50M–500M depending on traction at the time.

Deep personal commitment to the long-term mission of supporting independent local Businesses. Has stated intention to stay through at least the Series A.
