The 30-minute Business pitch

After discovery qualified them, this is the meeting. Six sections, ~30 minutes total. Goal: get a signed sign-up form by minute 30. The structure is field-tested by Sienna across 87 wins.

1. Open · 0-3 min

Greet, recap, set expectation
You"Hey [first name], thanks for the time. Last time we chatted you mentioned [their pain point — lunch traffic / younger customers / etc.]. I came back with real numbers from 3 Businesses your size in your category. Plan today: I'll spend 5 minutes hearing what's changed since we talked, 10 minutes walking through the LYMX model, 10 minutes on the math specifically for your Business, then 5 minutes on what sign-up looks like. Sound good?"
Why this worksYou set the agenda upfront so they're not surprised at minute 25 when you ask for the sign-up. You also reminded them why they took this meeting (their pain). You handed them the steering wheel for the first 5 min.

2. Their world · 3-8 min

Listen, validate, build rapport

The discovery questions you asked last time — re-ask the most important one, plus dig deeper on their stated pain.

You"You mentioned [pain point]. Walk me through what that looks like on a typical week. What have you tried? What's worked, what hasn't?"

Listen. Take notes (visibly — it shows respect). Ask follow-up questions but don't pitch yet. Common follow-ups:

  • "What does a slow day look like vs. your best day?"
  • "Who do you think your ideal customer is — and how do you know if you're getting them?"
  • "What would 'good' look like for your Business 6 months from now?"
Why this worksYou're building the case for LYMX in their head before you say a word about it. By minute 8, they should be thinking "I really need a solution to this." That's when you transition.

3. The LYMX story · 8-15 min

Brief, concrete, story-driven

Don't list features. Tell the story. Specifically: how the network was started, who's on it, why it works, and one concrete success story relevant to their pain.

You"LYMX started in late 2025 here in Vegas. Founded by Kenny Lin, who'd owned 12+ restaurants himself and watched the same loyalty problem play out everywhere — closed-loop punch cards work for retention but do nothing for acquisition.

The idea behind LYMX is simple: every local Business shares one loyalty rewards network. Customers earn LYMX rewards at the coffee shop, redeem at the salon, earn at the salon, redeem at the dim sum place. Every Business in the network is sending customers to every other Business. We have 218 Businesses on the network now and 8,400 active customers in Vegas.

Here's what that meant for [comparable Business]..."

Then tell ONE story tied to their pain. Brief — 60-90 seconds. Specific Business, specific result, specific mechanism.

You (story example)"Brew & Bean Coffee in Henderson — same owner profile as you, similar size. They joined LYMX February 2026. Their Tuesday lunch was their slowest day, doing about $200/hour from 11-3. They ran a 2x LYMX Tuesday lunch promo. Within 3 months Tuesday was up to $340/hour. They tracked it and 60% of the new traffic was from customers who first earned LYMX at OTHER Businesses, then came to Brew & Bean to redeem. The network was their growth channel, not their existing audience."
Why this worksYou named the Business (real, verifiable). You named numbers (specific, plausible). You named the mechanism (network → cross-redemption). You connected to their pain (lunch traffic). They can picture the same outcome.

4. The math for THEIR Business · 15-23 min

Calculator, real numbers

Open biz-revenue-calc on your laptop. Plug in their actual numbers based on what they told you. Walk through it line by line.

You"Let's plug in your numbers. Roughly what's your monthly revenue? [They say.] Average ticket size? [They say.] Customer count per day? [They say.] And what % is repeat vs. new? [They say.]

Okay, here's what LYMX looks like for you in year 1..."

Sample calculation — independent restaurant doing $50K/month

LYMX subscription$199/mo × 12 = $2,388
Sign-up fee (one-time, includes 3 mo free + launch kit)$850
LYMX issuance cost (~$0.05/$1 issued)~$3,000
Total annual cost~$5,838
Expected incremental revenue (12-18% lift typical)$60,000-90,000
Net contribution (year 1)+$54,000-84,000

Let them do the math in their head. Pause. Don't sell. They'll either say "that's significant" or push back on assumptions. Both are good.

Why this worksYou used THEIR numbers, not generic ones. Even if your assumptions are conservative, you anchored to a credible range. The phrase "expected incremental revenue" is more believable than "you'll make $X."

5. Sign-up walkthrough · 23-28 min

What's actually involved

Walk through the practical mechanics so they don't have unanswered questions blocking the close.

You"If we move forward today, here's what happens:

Today: we sign the application — takes 4 minutes, 3 pages.
This week: I activate your POS integration. About 8 minutes. We test 2-3 transactions live.
Within 7 days: your launch kit arrives — signage, training cards, free customer-acquisition email blast.
Day 8 onwards: you're live. Customers earning, you running normally.

Cost is $850 today, then $199/mo starting month 4 (3 months free included). That's it. Cancel anytime — 90-day wind-down, no lock-in."
Why this worksYou showed them the path is short and concrete. No fine print, no surprises. The 3 months free + 90-day money-back-guarantee makes the downside feel low.

6. Close · 28-30 min

Direct ask, no theater

Direct close. Ask plainly. Don't talk yourself out of the sale.

You (Option A — Direct close)"Want to do this? I have the application here. We can get you signed and live by next week."
You (Option B — Soft close if they hesitate)"What's the one thing standing between us moving forward today? Let me address that specifically."

If they raise an objection, go to partner-objections for the response. Most objections at this point are buyer's remorse, not real blockers.

If they say "let me think about it":

You"Of course. What specifically do you want to think through? I'd rather come back next week with answers to your real concerns than just do a generic follow-up."
Why this worksYou're respecting their decision-making while preventing the conversation from drifting into a soft maybe. Most "let me think about it" moments are real questions they haven't articulated.

Pre-meeting checklist

  • Reviewed your discovery notes — what was their stated pain
  • Picked the right comparable Business story (similar size + category + neighborhood)
  • Pre-loaded biz-revenue-calc on your laptop
  • Brought sign-up application (printed, or on tablet)
  • Charged tablet, working pen
  • Reviewed top 3 likely objections for this Business type
  • Pre-checked POS supported list — confirmed compatible
  • Allowed 60 minutes (30 meeting + 30 buffer for follow-up questions)

Post-meeting

  1. If signed: hand off to onboarding (check biz-onboarding-checklist)
  2. If not signed: log specific objection + next-step date in partner-crm
  3. Send same-day thank-you note (partner-followup-templates) — even if they said yes
  4. Mark in calendar: 90-day check-in if signed, 30-day re-touch if not
