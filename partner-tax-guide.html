Partner tax guide

The basics — how Partner income is taxed

LYMX Partner commissions are self-employment income. That's different from W-2 employee income in three big ways:

  1. You're responsible for paying both halves of FICA (Social Security + Medicare) — about 15.3% on top of regular income tax. The IRS calls this "self-employment tax."
  2. You don't have an employer withholding taxes. You pay them yourself, usually quarterly.
  3. You can deduct legitimate business expenses against your Partner income, which most W-2 employees can't.
✓

Plan for ~30% of gross commission going to taxes

For most Partners, between federal income tax (10–24%) + self-employment tax (15.3%) — roughly 25–35% of your gross commissions ends up as tax. Set aside 30% as a default.

Schedule C — your tax form

Partner income is reported on Schedule C (Profit or Loss from Business) of your federal tax return. The flow looks like this:

  1. Add up your gross commission (matches your 1099-NEC) → enter as gross receipts on Schedule C
  2. Subtract legitimate business expenses (see below) → calculate net profit
  3. Net profit flows to your 1040 as ordinary income, taxed at your marginal rate
  4. Same net profit also flows to Schedule SE (Self-Employment Tax) — you owe 15.3% on top of income tax

If your net Partner profit is under $400, you don't owe self-employment tax — but you still owe regular income tax on it.

Common deductions

Anything you spend that's ordinary and necessary for your Partner work is potentially deductible. Some common ones:

🚗Mileage

Driving to prospect Businesses, training events, networking. 70¢/mile in 2026 (verify current rate). Track in a mileage log.

📱Phone

Business-use portion of your phone bill. Most Partners deduct 30–50%.

💻Software & tools

CRM, scheduling tools, design tools, even Notion/Trello if used for prospect tracking.

📚Training & events

Conferences, books, courses, LYMX-organized training events you traveled to.

🍽️Business meals

50% deductible. Includes meeting a prospect for coffee or lunch. Keep receipts + a note about the business purpose.

🏠Home office

Dedicated home workspace, calculated by % of home sq ft. Strict criteria — read up before claiming.

💼Equipment

Laptop, tablet, business cards, branded clothing if used solely for Partner work.

🌐Marketing & ads

Paid social, business cards, website, professional photos, anything you spend to get prospects.

📋

Keep receipts & track everything

The IRS doesn't accept "I think I spent…" Keep digital copies of all receipts (Quickbooks, Wave, or even a Google Drive folder works). Track mileage in an app — MileIQ, Stride, or just a spreadsheet. The hour/week you spend on this saves you thousands at tax time.

Tip: open a separate "tax savings" account

The day commission hits your account, transfer 30% to a separate savings account. When the quarterly due date arrives, the money is sitting there. Most Partners who get caught by tax bills are the ones who didn't separate the money.

5 common mistakes Partners make

1. Spending all the commission, then getting hit with a tax bill in April

Classic mistake — you earn $4k in commissions, spend it like income, then in April you owe $1,200 in tax you didn't set aside. Solution: 30% transfer rule above.

2. Missing the Q1 quarterly deadline

If you started in October 2025 but didn't pay anything until April 2026, you may owe a small underpayment penalty for Q4 2025. It's usually small ($20–80) — but it's avoidable. As soon as you become a Partner, set quarterly deadlines on your calendar.

3. Not tracking mileage

If you drive 200 miles a month for prospecting, that's 2,400 miles/year. At 70¢/mile, that's $1,680 in deductions you missed if you didn't track. Get a mileage app on day 1.

4. Trying to deduct things that aren't actually business expenses

Just because you bought it doesn't mean it's deductible. The standard is "ordinary and necessary for your Partner work." Coffee with a friend who isn't a prospect? Not deductible. New AirPods you use 95% for personal? Not fully deductible. Be honest — the IRS audits aggressive deductions.

5. Forgetting state taxes

Federal tax isn't the only one. Most states (including Nevada — wait, Nevada has no state income tax actually) have state income tax. If you're in California or Arizona, you'll owe state too. Check your state's quarterly schedule separately.

FAQ

I made $400 last year. Do I need to file?

If your net Partner profit is $400+, you owe self-employment tax and need to file Schedule SE. Even if your income otherwise wouldn't require filing, this might.

Is my $500 Business sign-up bonus taxable?

Yes. All Partner commissions including the one-time bonus are taxable income reported on your 1099-NEC.

What about LYMX I earned as a customer in my own wallet — is that taxable?

No. LYMX you earn as a customer (not as a Partner) is treated as a discount, not income. It's not on a 1099 and you don't owe tax on it. This applies to LYMX you earn at any Business including your own.

Should I form an LLC for my Partner work?

Most Partners with $<$50k in annual commission don't need one. The pass-through tax treatment of an LLC isn't different from sole proprietorship for federal purposes (state may differ). But an LLC can offer some liability protection and looks more professional. Talk to a tax pro if you're earning $30k+/yr.

Can LYMX hold tax for me?

No. As a self-employed independent contractor, you're responsible for your own taxes. LYMX cannot withhold federal or state income tax from your commission payments.
